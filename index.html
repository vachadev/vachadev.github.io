Empowering bastis with resources for last 25 years

Vacha emerged as a women’s group during the 1980s. Initiated by women active in the women’s rights movement, It started as a resource centre to address the need for space for discourse, resources, and collective action. It was established and registered as a Trust in 1990.

Since 1995 Vacha has focused on working with adolescent girls and boys with a special focus on girls. Vacha looks at its work with adolescents as a preventive measure against the creation of vulnerabilities in women due to lack of education, exposure, and opportunity. Vacha believes that empowered girls and sensitized boys have a better chance of developing into adults who value equality and become productive citizens.

Our Work

Vacha focuses on issues of girls through educational programmes, resource creation, research, training, campaigns, and networking. A strong belief in gender equality and rights forms the core of Vacha’s work.

Vacha’s interventions are targeted towards marginalized youth in the second decade of life. These are the young people who live in poor neighborhoods popularly called slums and referred to by Vacha and people inhabiting these as basti.

Though both girls and boys participate in our activities, we primarily work with girls. Vacha also advocates for girls and women by participating in various networks and campaigns, organising seminars and conferences, and production of publications.

Currently, our interventions are in 18 basti centres in Mumbai and Thane Districts.

Putting our funds to work

Your support empowers individuals, strengthens families, and makes our community a better place to live. We initiate youth groups in bastis that take action in their neighborhoods on civic issues such as the safety of girls and women.

Our Covid-19 Response

Vacha has been actively working towards intersectionally deprived girls’ empowerment through diverse interventions. Last year, the onset of COVID-19 pandemic and the subsequent lockdown had been a challenging situation for our program participants.

Parents of our programme participants work in the informal sector and even during normal times, they barely manage to have 2 meals in a day. The lockdown situation further disrupted their economic conditions as their earning members lost their jobs and their earning opportunities came to a standstill.
